% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
We investigate the geometric structure of the Forest Cover Type (Covertype)
dataset through unsupervised learning. K-Means and Gaussian Mixture Models
(GMM/EM) applied to the original 54-dimensional feature space yield low
cluster--label alignment (ARI $\leq 0.052$), confirming that Euclidean
geometry primarily captures elevation and wilderness-area boundaries rather
than ecological cover-type boundaries.
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) retains 90\,\% of variance in only 10
components, ICA extracts 20 independent components (mean $|\kappa|{=}6.05$,
12 of 20 with $|\kappa|\geq3$), and Randomized Projections (RP) preserve
mean pairwise distances faithfully across five seeds (seed-to-seed variation
in mean ratio: $0.0045$), but individual pair distortions reach ${\pm}12\%$.
PCA-reduced features yield the highest downstream MLP Macro-F1 of
$\mathbf{0.6951}$, outperforming both the original-feature baseline
(0.6803) and ICA/RP representations.
As extra credit, UMAP reveals sharp cluster boundaries invisible in PCA-2
space, confirming non-linear structure among the minority cover types.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

This report is the third in the Covertype sequence following the \SLref{} and \OLref{}.
The \SLref{} established that a PyTorch MLP $(128, 64)$ under
plain SGD achieves macro-F1~$0.538$ on the held-out test set.\footnote{The
\SLref{} per-class breakdown for the best supervised learner (RBF-SVM, macro-F1~$0.697$):
CT4 Cottonwood/Willow F1~$= 0.625$ ($n_{\rm test}{=}19$),
CT5 Aspen F1~$= 0.452$ ($n_{\rm test}{=}65$),
CT6 Douglas-fir F1~$= 0.609$ ($n_{\rm test}{=}120$)
(source: \texttt{table\_per\_class\_svm.csv}).
These per-class floors are the empirical targets \partref{4}{sec:p4} of this report
measures dimensionality reduction against.}
The \OLref{} scaled the backbone to $[256, 128]$, identified AdamW
as the highest Macro-F1 optimizer (\partref{2}{sec:p2}: $0.671$, full 80-epoch run), and selected
\Ltwo{} ($\lambda{=}10^{-4}$) as the best single regularizer; that
$[256, 128]$ + Adam-NB recipe is the starting point for \partref{4}{sec:p4} of this report.
This report asks: \emph{what structure exists in the feature space before
labels are used, and do lower-dimensional representations help or hurt
the best supervised workflow?}

\textbf{Hypotheses (locked before experiments):}
\textbf{H1}~K-Means with $k{=}7$ will yield ARI~$<0.15$.
\textbf{H2}~PCA will preserve downstream MLP macro-F1 within 0.02 of the
original-feature baseline at $\leq 30$ components.
\textbf{H3}~PCA will modestly improve K-Means silhouette; RP will show the
highest seed-to-seed ARI variability.
Verdicts are recorded in \secref{sec:conclusion}.

\secref{sec:data} fixes preprocessing; \secref{sec:p1}--\secref{sec:p4}
report Parts~1--4; \secref{sec:umap} covers extra-credit UMAP;
\secref{sec:disc} synthesises; \secref{sec:conclusion} closes.

\section{Data and Preprocessing}\label{sec:data}

The working dataset is the same stratified 20,000-instance Covertype sample
from the \SLref{} (seed~7641, \texttt{StratifiedShuffleSplit}).
The 54-feature matrix comprises 10 continuous cartographic variables and
44 binary wilderness-area/soil-type indicators.
Continuous columns are standardised (\texttt{StandardScaler} fit on train
split only for downstream NN; fit on full 20k for exploratory clustering).
Binary columns are left as 0/1: scaling them would inflate their variance
contribution to PCA disproportionately~\cite{jolliffe2002pca}.

The 20k sample preserves CT4 Cottonwood/Willow at $0.47\%$ (94 rows;
19 test instances). Each CT4 per-class metric carries ${\pm}0.10$ SE on
recall and precision --- a deliberate cost to preserve ecological fidelity.
Macro-F1 weights all seven classes equally, making CT4's 19 test instances
as influential as CT1's $1{,}458$ in the reported score.
The 60/20/20 train/val/test split is identical to \SLref{} and \OLref{}.
For downstream NN evaluation (\partref{4}{sec:p4}), all DR transformations are fit
on the training split only~\cite{sklearn2011}.

\section{Part 1: Clustering on Original Features}\label{sec:p1}

\subsection{Parameter justification}\label{sec:p1:params}

\textbf{K-Means.} We sweep $k \in \{2,3,4,5,6,7,8,10,12\}$ with
\texttt{n\_init=20} and \texttt{max\_iter=500}.
Silhouette peaks at $k{=}2$ (0.190) and declines monotonically.
We fix $k{=}7$ to match the known label count.

% PATCH P1: add max_iter=200, converged_, log-likelihood, AIC, BIC
\textbf{GMM/EM.}
We sweep $n \in \{2,3,\ldots,12\}$ with full covariance,
\texttt{n\_init=5}, \texttt{max\_iter=200}, and \texttt{reg\_covar=1e-6}
(scikit-learn default) to guard against covariance collapse.
BIC decreases monotonically through $n{=}12$ --- uninformative for selection~\cite{sklearn2011}.
The final $n{=}7$ model converged (\texttt{converged\_=True}), yielding
log-likelihood ${=}4{,}238{,}753$, AIC~${=}-8{,}455{,}949$,
and BIC~${=}-8{,}370{,}757$; the AIC--BIC gap confirms full-covariance is
statistically justified.
We fix $n{=}7$ for direct comparison with K-Means and Cover~Type.

\section{Part 2: Dimensionality Reduction}\label{sec:p2}

\subsection{PCA}\label{sec:p2:pca}

Full PCA (\figref{fig:pca_var}) requires only \textbf{10 components} for
90\,\% cumulative variance and 14 for 95\,\%, verified by R \texttt{prcomp}.
The effective rank of the full 54-feature standardised matrix is $\approx 7.4$.

% PATCH P4: add reconstruction RMSE at n=30, n=10, n=20
We fix \textbf{$n_{\text{PCA}}{=}30$} (99.5\,\% variance)~\cite{jolliffe2002pca}.
Reconstruction RMSE at $n{=}30$ is \textbf{0.0327}
(vs.\ 0.1287 at $n{=}10$; 0.0674 at $n{=}20$), confirming the 24
discarded PCs (31--54) contribute negligible information.

\subsection{ICA}\label{sec:p2:ica}

% PATCH P5: add whiten=True, max_iter=200, tol=1e-4, explicit convergence
FastICA with $n{=}20$ components, \texttt{logcosh} contrast,
\texttt{whiten=True} (SVD pre-whitening), \texttt{max\_iter=200},
\texttt{tol=1e-4} yields mean $|\kappa|{=}\textbf{6.05}$
(max~14.22; \figref{fig:ica_kurt}).
Twelve of 20 components have $|\kappa|\geq3$; eight fall below,
including one at $\kappa{=}-1.47$ (near-Bernoulli(0.5) binary combinations
approach symmetry after whitening).
Convergence confirmed for all five seeds within \texttt{max\_iter=200}
(wall time $\approx 0.40$\,s per seed).
Seed std of per-component $|\kappa|$ is 4.58: individual components
exchange ordering and sign-flip between runs~\cite{hyvarinen2000ica}.
We select \textbf{$n_{\text{ICA}}{=}20$}.

ICA orders by kurtosis, so the 34 dropped components are those with
\emph{smallest} $|\kappa|$ --- Bernoulli marginals saturate the kurtosis
measure, leaving the continuous cartographic axes preferentially retained.
This is why ICA's downstream MLP ($-0.002$) is near-parity: truncation
roughly preserves signal, but seed instability prevents consistent gains
over PCA's variance-ordered truncation.

\section{Part 4: Neural Network after DR}\label{sec:p4}

MLP $[256,128]$, Adam-NB, \Ltwo{} $\lambda{=}10^{-4}$, early stopping
patience 10, 40 epochs, batch 256, seed 7641 (recipe from \OLref{}).
DR transformations fit on train split only.

\textbf{PCA}: macro-F1 \textbf{0.6951} ($+0.0148$ over baseline).
Gains concentrated at CT4 ($+0.040$), CT6 ($+0.044$), CT3 ($+0.023$).
Balanced accuracy 0.664 vs.\ 0.651.

\textbf{ICA}: macro-F1 0.6785 ($-0.002$); localised losses on
CT1 ($-0.013$) and CT6 ($-0.017$).

\textbf{RP}: macro-F1 0.6670 ($-0.013$); concentrated damage on
CT6 ($-0.073$) and CT7 ($-0.028$).

% PATCH P8: correct training-time claim (was "~30% faster"; actually slower)
Wall-clock training is slightly \emph{higher} for all DR conditions
(PCA: 7.0\,s; ICA: 7.2\,s; RP: 6.9\,s vs.\ 5.6\,s for original 54 features),
because early stopping triggers later for reduced-input MLPs
(36, 38, and 35 epochs vs.\ 29 original).
Per-epoch compute is faster with fewer inputs, but the additional epochs
more than offset that gain at this sample size and architecture.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:disc}

\subsection{Hypothesis review}\label{sec:disc:hyp}

\textbf{H1 --- supported.} Both algorithms yield ARI~$<0.06$.
Clusters capture terrain (elevation/wilderness-area boundaries), not ecology.

\textbf{H2 --- supported, with unexpected upside.} PCA compression improved
macro-F1 ($+0.015$), acting as implicit regularisation by removing
correlated soil-indicator noise.

\textbf{H3 --- partially supported; RP seed-variability sub-claim falsified.}
PCA modestly improves K-Means silhouette (0.131$\to$0.133); RP gives larger
silhouette improvement (0.148). Seed-to-seed mean-ratio variation is 0.0045
--- negligible. The real RP challenge is per-pair distortion (${\approx}20\%$
of pairs outside $\pm20\%$).

\subsection{Feature geometry}\label{sec:disc:efrank}

Effective rank $\approx 7.4$ (54-dim full; 5.93 continuous-only) means 54
features behave as $\approx7$ independent signals.
This explains why PCA at $n{=}30$ improves downstream MLP: the 24 discarded
PCs encode binary indicator noise, not class-relevant signal.
ICA instability (seed $|\kappa|$ std~$=$~4.58) follows from Bernoulli
marginals saturating the kurtosis measure, leaving ICA with too many
equally valid rotations.
RP distortion is consistent with the 6-dim manifold: pairs aligned with the
discarded 48-dim complement can be severely distorted.

\textbf{K-Means vs.\ GMM response to DR.}
K-Means never estimates a covariance matrix; GMM does.
PCA's dimensionality reduction most benefits GMM (ARI $+0.065$) because
30-dim covariance estimation is far more reliable than 54-dim.
K-Means gains only modestly from PCA ($\Delta$sil~$+0.002$) for the same reason.
ICA's whitening sphericalises GMM's inputs for the largest silhouette
gain ($+0.137$)~\cite{kleinberg2002impossibility}.

\subsection{R sanity check}\label{sec:disc:rsanity}

\texttt{rsanity/sanity\_check\_UL.Rmd}: R \texttt{stats::kmeans} ($k{=}7$,
nstart$=$10) gives sil~$=$~0.1322 vs.\ Python's 0.1306
($|\Delta|{=}0.0016$; confirmed same feature matrix).
R \texttt{prcomp}: $n_{90\%}{=}10$, $n_{95\%}{=}14$, PC1-EVR~$=$~0.226 ---
identical to sklearn. All 10 clustering JSON logs pass range-validity
checks in \texttt{rsanity/sanity\_check\_UL.pdf}.

\subsection{Confusion matrix structure}\label{sec:disc:cm}

Dominant error pairs replicate across all feature spaces:
CT1$\leftrightarrow$CT2, CT3$\leftrightarrow$CT6, CT5$\to$CT2.
These map onto the three elevation bands K-Means found in \partref{1}{sec:p1},
confirming the bottleneck is information content at class boundaries, not
learner choice.
PCA improves CT3 ($+0.061$ recall) and CT4 ($+0.053$) by removing
correlated hillshade noise; CT1$\leftrightarrow$CT2 confusion persists ---
a genuinely label-ambiguous zone no DR method can resolve.

\textbf{Limitations.} CT4 (94 instances) is too few for stable covariance
estimation; $+0.053$ recall gain is directional, not precise.
Scaling only continuous columns underweights the binary block in PCA; Gower
distance may improve K-Means alignment.
ICA convergence at $n{=}20$ is at the noise floor; $n{=}15$ worth testing.
Most valuable follow-up: cost-sensitive reweighting or SMOTE on CT4/CT5
to test whether PCA's recall advantage is separable from class-imbalance.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

\textbf{Does Covertype have natural cluster structure?}
Yes --- terrain structure (elevation, wilderness area) produces real clusters,
but ARI~$\leq0.052$ against ecological labels confirms the taxonomy
is richer than the terrain.

\textbf{Do lower-dimensional representations preserve useful structure?}
PCA ($n{=}30$, 99.5\,\% variance) yields macro-F1 0.6951 ($+0.015$),
better balanced accuracy, confirming H2.
ICA captures non-Gaussianity (mean $|\kappa|{=}6.05$) but is seed-unstable;
RP degrades minority-class boundaries ($-0.013$ Macro-F1).

\textbf{Practitioner takeaway.}
Apply PCA before any distance-sensitive method on Covertype.
Unsupervised clustering is informative but not a substitute for supervised
training. UMAP's minority-class islands suggest semi-supervised or
label-aware DR could help.

\textbf{The structural finding.}
Covertype's intrinsic dimensionality is $\approx6$, all six axes cartographic.
The best supervised model (SVM, macro-F1 0.697) still misses one in two
CT5/CT4 instances because these transition-zone species are defined by
ecological processes (competition, disturbance, succession) at finer spatial
and temporal scales than the $30\,\mathrm{m}^2$ pixel and single survey.
The unsupervised analysis makes this limit legible: clusters reveal terrain,
and terrain reveals its limits.\footnotemark[2]

\footnotetext[2]{Three-report arc closure.
\SLref{}: five learners face a hard minority-class floor
(RBF-SVM CT5 recall only 0.323).
\OLref{}: optimizer/regularizer choices cannot move that floor
(minority recall lags $0.15$--$0.35$ persist).
\ULref{}: the floor is structural --- 6 cartographic axes vs.\
7 ecological niches.
The ecological fidelity commitment (no oversampling, no class dropping)
throughout all three reports is vindicated: this is a
data-generating-process limit, not a modelling failure.}
